    Roman Miletitch, Jérémie Cortial

    Draw/Play: digital interacting on paper

    First there was Flippaper, a project between a drafting table and a pinball. Take a few felt pens, draw a few shapes, press SCAN and ta-dah!, your drawing lights up. And it doesn't stop there: with the help of smoke, mirrors and a bit of tech, a ball appears on your drawing and reacts to it. A whole pinball emerges before your eyes. Through a language of shapes and colors, you're in charge of the gameplay and the look of your pinball. Our aim is to release to the public the underlying tech that made this project a reality. This means both an open source code and an easy access to open hardware kits. We hope this will allow people to easily develop and play with this new interaction possibilities. During out time together, we will present this take on interactivity, Flippaper as its first result, and finally the library itself: its usages and possibilities. Jéremie Cortial and Roman Miletitch are the left and right arms of Papertronics, aiming to find more natural and physical (who said carnal?) ways to interact with new technologies. Pigments on canvas was used by cavemen, so it felt good enough for us. Felt pen, paint or colored dot stickers on papers, colored food syrup on thin white waffle, graff on a wall, body paint on naked bodies... draw with any means necessary and grant your drawing life by pressing the magical SCAN button. What was static becomes dynamic, alive and utterly fun to play! A whole new world of messy interactions opens up. We're the welcoming committee.

    A quest for a Git visualizing tool for designers

    Where is this tool or set of tools that could improve a designers workflow using Git and open the possibilities of distributed visual collaboration ? Since the presentation « Design with Git » at LGM in 2013 and a first prototype built during the Interactivos that followed, the project had been stalled for a while looking for opportunities to develop it further. Then this past year brought me a lot more free time, so I decided to take a fresh approach to the problem and get back to the drawing board in another attempt to solve it. This talk will present you the research done so far, how some others have had their go at it, the strength and weaknesses of what exists and the development of a new platform : « Git for designers ».

He regularly collaborates with artists in the world of entertainment, music, plastic and digital arts. His latest open licensed projects include the [loveMachine], a bot that likes everything your friends do on Facebook, WORD WARS, a generative video series on Youtube and the RaspiVJ, an application that turns the Raspberry Pi into a lightweight portable VJ plateform. All projects have been released under libre licences. He is also the co-founder of : Blender-Brussels, monthly workshops around experimental Python coding in Blender, F/LAT, an organization dedicated to education and research in free/libre and open source technologies for artists, and Libre Objet, a collective of hackers and designers promoting libre licensing applied to industrial design.

    Michael Murtaugh, Manuel Schmalstieg

    Building the LGM (Video) Archive

    In the last year, there has been work on creating a video archive for the collected audio/visual recordings of 10 years of Libre Graphics Meetings. Keeping legacy online resources alive is challenging, particularly for smaller-scale free culture organisations like the Libre Graphics Meeting. Changes occur not only to technical formats, capabilities and styles, but also to the surrounding social structures with changing contributors, skills, and desires. There is a diversity of kinds of documents produced and gathered (webpages of various vintage and provenance, slides, audio/video recordings, photos, external pages and social media). Currently organisations are forced to balance between maintaining resources in their own CMS and relying on external resources such as Google and the Internet archive. Are there technical means of embracing the distributed and diverse nature of the online video archive that don't risk falling between the perils of a single point of failure and over-reliance on the shifting interests of commercial (and often proprietary) clouds? This workshop is a practical opportunity to improve the current state of the archive (helping to tag) and to share the files (the LGM hardrive), as well a moment to reflect on the state of online publishing and emerging possibilities for digital archiving. More information (and links): https://pad.constantvzw.org/p/lgm-video-archive
    Organised by: Michael Murtaugh & Peter Westenberg: Constant / Active Archives, https://activearchives.org Manuel Schmalstieg: Greyscale Press / ms-studio, https://ms-studio.net

    lxlxl Studio , María Fernández, Rodolfo García

    Streamers’ Corner. Open tool for audiovisual demonstrations.

    Intervention in public spaces with live audiovisuals based on smartphones involves an innovative proposal which connects the social aspects with appropriation and dynamization of spaces from a cultural transformation point of view in keeping with modern times we live in. Streamers' Corner overcomes the difficulties of developing these shows without any need for previous production, using present technology in smartphones, for the purpose of recovering public spaces for audiovisual art. An electronic unplugged, in essence, with a minimum deployment and without production problems, previous tests, logistics, or noises. Its scheme is the following: -Artists broadcast with their laptops to a computer which is in charge for processing live sound and images, convert them in a valid format for mobile phones, and for launch it through a private WIFI in a live atmosphere. -The user connects from his smarthphone or tablet to that private WIFI and inmediately will listen the music and watch the real time sincronized visuals through his device. Since the communications are being transferred in a private environment, quality is very good and have lower latency levels. The result is that any person with a mobile phone with WIFI (smartphone (Android, IOS), tablet, iPad) and a pair of headphones can actually enjoy a audiovisual experience never raised until now, in environments that could not ever used before. But the concept goes further beyond, since it is not necessary any power source. Artists use laptops batteries and an portable UPS to feed the communication electronics. So it is possible to set up a live show very quickly without practically any production.
    lxlxl (a.k.a. Thr3hold) are Rodolfo Lillo (1971) and María Fernández (1984). They create a collective of digital creation in 2008 in Asturias (north of Spain) bringing together common visions and artistic worries from its members. Having a solid background on graphic, industrial design and technology, their pieces of work are based on exploration and experimentation of new visual tendencies keeping the maximum simplicity and conceptual abstraction of the compositions. They divided their work in two different lines of creation: Thr3hold as laboratory of real time visual makers for experimental electronic musicians and lxlxl as platform to develop new concepts for installations, products or creation tools always related to new media. They also organized Trendelenburg, an open platform focused to spread digital culture in public spaces. The main event is a free annual meeting to be held every March in public spaces in the region of Asturias.

    For the Libre Graphics Meeting Hackers & Designers proposes an open walk-in workshop/publishing station.     Michael Murtaugh, Nicolas Malevé

    Scandanavian Institute for Computational Vandalism

    Today "computer vision" techniques are pervasive, integrated in smartphone cameras, surveillance systems, and cloud-based image search and tagging. Within the last 15 years, CV engineers increasingly use so-called "visual vocabularies" -- statistically determined clusters of algorithmically determined visual "features" -- to index and order image collections. But what precisely are these features in visual terms? What can visual art practices offer to (and receive from) these techniques? This project is inspired by the Scandinavian Institute of Comparative Vandalism (SICV) founded by Asger Jorn shortly after leaving the Situationist International in 1961. The SICV was an art and research association experimenting with the practice of collage, the forces of photography, image archives, and political imaginaries. Today Computational Vandalism challenges the assumptions at the basis of computer vision, the discipline that programmatically ties together the visual and the mnemonic. Work of the SICV has taken several forms: software "probes" (documented in blog form), installations, and publications. We will present an overview of the work, addressing the questions: What does computer vision have to offer to creative graphic design practitioners? When does a cheesy effect become a scientific instrument? How does an instrument of surveillance become a digital paintbrush? How is an image more than the sum of it's pixels?
    Michael Murtaugh (1972, USA) designs and researches community databases, interactive documentary, and tools for new forms of reading and writing online. He teaches on the Masters Degree programme in Media Design and Communication at the Piet Zwart Institute in Rotterdam. Nicolas Malevé (1969, Belgium) is an artist, software programmer and data activist developing multimedia projects and web applications for and with cultural organizations. His current research work is focused on cartography, information structures, metadata, archives and algorithms.

    Stefan Zellmann

    Visionaray: a modern C++ framework for prototyping ray tracing kernels

    Visionaray is a framework to rapidly prototype algorithms that are based on shooting rays through 3-D space. The following code excerpt shows what a simple, single-bounce real-time ray tracing algorithm may look like with Visionaray: sched.frame([=](ray_type r) -> color_type { auto hit_rec = closest_hit( r, primitives.begin(), primitives.end()); if (any(hit_rec.hit)) { auto color = ...; // Calculate radiance return select( hit_rec.hit, color, background_color); } return background_color; }, sparams); sched is a scheduler instance that, depending on its specific type, generates primary rays, performs ray traversal based on so called ray traversal kernels, and writes the traversal result to a virtual or hardware frame buffer. The lambda function parameter to sched's frame() member function is a ray traversal kernel which is written in platform independent C++ and is compiled to highly optimized machine code for the target platform -- e.g. CPUs with SIMD and multi-threading, GPGPUs with nVidia CUDA, or whole PC clusters with MPI. Kernels can be reused across platforms! The user is expected to write custom kernels. While doing so, she has access to a set of optimized intrinsics for acceleration data structure traversal (e.g. closest_hit() from the example above), 1-D, 2-D, or 3-D texture access with higher-order interpolation, physically based surface shaders, or ray packet traversal for coherent rays (e.g. any() or select()). Prototyping ray tracing algorithms with Visionaray is as easy as can be because the user can concentrate on the traversal algorithm rather than having to care about platform specific restrictions. This is accomplished through tight integration with libraries such as STL, Boost and Thrust. When prototyping algorithms, using Visionaray is as simple as using a DSL. However, when optimization for a specific platform is necessary, the user may benefit from the flexibility of writing native C++ code and having access to platform specific libraries and language features. Visionaray integrates with the scientific visualization software COVISE by means of a plugin for the OpenSceneGraph-based virtual reality renderer OpenCOVER. This relatively new side project will bridge the worlds of real-time global illumination and virtual reality.
    Stefan Zellmann studied information systems until 2009 and received his PhD in computer science from the University of Cologne, Germany in 2014. He has an expertise in scientific visualization and high-performance computing, but is also an enthusiast for photorealistic 3-D rendering and physically based lighting simulation. He is currently with the computer graphics group at the University of Cologne and participated in several research projects where he developed imaging and graphics applications with C++. He contributed to several open source graphics and visualization applications such as the volume rendering package DeskVOX (https://github.com/deskvox/deskvox), or the scientific visualization software COVISE (https://github.com/hlrs-vis/covise). For about a year and a half, he is the lead architect and programmer of the C++-based ray tracing framework Visionaray.

    Yorik van Havre

    Architectural design, BIM and FreeCAD

    BIM stands for Building Information Modeling, the new heaven promised to architects, destinated to replace older, traditional CAD and unify all parts of a building project. The precise definition of BIM stays until today a bit blurry, and many still wonder where the big changes are. However, for the first time in a world traditionally ruled by software vendors, BIM is born with a foot in the open-source world, thanks, mainly, to an open file format, the IFC format, and the very active community around it, who, step by step, are turning this format into an unavoidable world standard. This has many important consequences, a big one being the possibility to free the architectural design process from the paradigms fed by major software vendors, IFC being extremely down-to-earth, and allow architects to rethink their tools and workflows. Many open-source projects have blossomed around it, and with FreeCAD, we have a possibility to effectively deliver professional-level BIM work with a full A-to-Z control over our data, our methods and our workflow, the open-source way. In this talk I will do my best to illustrate a bit of that story.
    Yorik is architect, lives in Brazil, is one of the principals of the https://www.uncreated.net architecture collective, also participates to other open studios such as https://www.openingdesign.com and one of the main developers of FreeCAD (https://www.freecadweb.org) (which only means that he's been hanging around there for some time, everybody can become main developer of FreeCAD!), where he is mostly responsible for the architecture module.

    Mark Meagher, Phil Langley

    topoBIM: A 3D editor of topological data for early stage architectural design

    The early stage of architectural design is characterized by limited information, rapid cycles of design development, and input from multiple stakeholders. This is a stage when proprietary computer aided design tools offer limited value: Building Information Modelling (or BIM), the industry standard software for creating and sharing 3D models with added building information, is seldom used in early design due to the cost and resource-intensiveness of the software and the high level of detail required to produce a workable model. This presentation describes the development of topoBIM: a lightweight, web-based software for viewing 3D BIM models and adding topological connections between spaces. Topological connections that can be used to represent multiple types of building information including connectedness, adjacency, movement of people, and heating and cooling services. The talk will focus on the use of the topological data structure as a way to maintain the abstraction and simplicity required in the early stage of design while retaining a local understanding of spatial relationships. I will also discuss the use of web based technologies as a means of widening collaboration and participatory potential within the early stage design process.
    Mark Meagher is a lecturer at the University of Sheffield School of Architecture, where he is a member of the Digital Design and Performance research group. His teaching and research focus on applications of building data, open source tools and bespoke software development in the architectural design process. Previously he was a researcher in the Media and Design Lab at EPFL in Lausanne, Switzerland; and in the Center for Design Informatics at Harvard University in Cambridge, USA. He is editor with Peter Blundell Jones of ‘Architecture and Movement’, a book investigating the experience and representation of movement in the built environment.

    “Artivity”, a tool for documenting creative practice in art and design

    Much of the appreciation of artistic and design work focuses on the finished output. However, increasingly, the value in such work is encapsulated in the process that leads to the finished output and not the output itself. Artivity is a software tool that captures the creative process alongside the finished output. Unlike other documentation methods which require input from the artist or designer, Artivity runs in the background and it does not interfere with the creative process. Artivity collects data about the online resources artists use combined with data about the way they use creative software. Artivity is useful for art and design historians who wish to interpret past work. It can help artists to self-archive their work. It can be used in teaching to explain and assess technique on creative software. Finally, Artivity data can help developers improve their creative software applications. Following an overview of the project, in this presentation we will explain how Artivity works and the type of data that it produces. We will also show some examples of data produced by artists using Artivity as well as our plans for future development.

    Ferrero Andrea

    PhotoFlow, a non-destructive layer-based RAW image editor

    PhotoFlow is a novel Open Source editor for RAW and raster images. Unlike other exiting Open Source RAW editors, PhotoFlow's workflow is based on the layers paradigm, pretty much like The GIMP. However, in the PhotoFlow case layers are fully non-destructive, allowing for unlimited adjustment of the tool parameters. Local adjustments are also possible through layer opacity masks. PhotoFlow supports most of the existing Bayer-based digital cameras, and provides a quite extended set of editing tools. Very recently, the software's been adapted to serve as a GIMP 2.9 plug-in, allowing to open and process RAW files and send the image to GIMP in 32-bits floating point format.
    I'm an amateur photographer and a Linux enthusiast since many years, and I write scientific programs in my daily work. In my spare time, I enjoy writing image processing code, with the hope of providing a better image editing experience for the whole Open Source community.

    Mark Meagher, Phil Langley

    Working with spatial data in early stage architectural design: Software development challenges and opportunities

    Building Information Modelling (BIM) is an architectural design paradigm used to structure and communicate spatial (3D) building data. There is a need in early stage architectural design for bespoke software that allows designers to accomplish specific tasks and perform analyses based on data from the BIM model, without the limitations associated with proprietary BIM software. In this workshop we will introduce challenges and invite discussion of solutions for the development of an open source BIM. Bespoke applications based on FLOSS software can extend access to data produced in proprietary BIM software to multiple stakeholders in the design process, and can be used to perform specific design tasks not provided by proprietary software. To introduce this topic we will present the example of topoBIM, a web-based application for viewing BIM models and editing topological data that can be used to capture multiple types of building information. This software was rapidly developed with limited resources using existing FLOSS applications and libraries for web-based building information model hosting (BIMServer) and visualization of BIM models based on WebGL (BIMSurfer).
    Mark Meagher is a lecturer at the University of Sheffield School of Architecture, where he is a member of the Digital Design and Performance research group. His teaching and research focus on applications of building data, open source tools and bespoke software development in the architectural design process.     Future of OpenRaster

    OpenRaster (.ora) is an open format for two-dimensional layered bitmap graphics. OpenRaster is supported by many art programs for data interchange, including Krita, GIMP, and Scribus, and it is the primary file format used by MyPaint.     Introducing an OpenHardware Spectrograph: ColorHug+

    The original ColorHug colorimeter device was designed in 2011, and then in 2014 we released an upgrade called ColorHug2. We've sold over 3 thousand OpenHardware devices to artists, photographers and animators who care passionately about free hardware. Whilst ColorHug and ColorHug2 colorimeters have calibrated many monitors over the years, they could not not measure ink color or surface patches which limited the free software color calibration workflow drastically. ColorHug+ is a new project that aims to solve this problem for people that care about free hardware. The device is a linear spectrograph and has sufficient resolution to build ICC color profiles for scanners, printers and displays. Spot measurements can be taken, and due to a switched UV illuminant, paper white can be accurately calculated for an accurate print preview. I've spent the last 2 years on-and-off working on this device, and now have several prototypes. In the presentation I will talk about the journey and the perils of OpenHardware, and what I think the future holds. I'll include lots of photos and not so many words. I'll also allow time at the end for questions and also bring some hardware for people to play with.
    Richard has over 10 years of experience developing open source software. He is the maintainer of GNOME Software, PackageKit, GNOME Packagekit, GNOME Power Manager, GNOME Color Manager, colord, fwupd, and UPower and also contributes to many other projects and open source standards. Richard has three main areas of interest on the free desktop, color management, package management, and power management. Richard graduated a few years ago from the University of Surrey with a Masters in Electronics Engineering. He now works for Red Hat in the desktop group, and also manages a company selling open source calibration equipment. Richard's outside interests include taking photos and eating good food.
